I
Better Than the First Time

His father gave him the trip as a graduation gift — Egypt again, for two again. Roy didn't argue with the choice of country. The first time had shown it was good there; the second was supposed to show it was even better. They took a higher-class hotel — the difference was felt from the first few minutes. More space, different service, a different feel to the place itself.

Breakfast came with fresh honeycomb. Every few days — as if by itself. These things don't get explained and they don't get planned, they just happen and stay in memory longer than most things.

Iva looked at everything differently than the first time — more confident, calmer, with that particular ease of someone who already knows where they are and why. Roy knew too. That's exactly why they'd come back.

· · ·

Andy found them himself — found out which hotel, showed up the following evening in his old Mitsubishi Lancer. Same car, same person — as if no time at all had passed between the first visit and this one. They hugged at the entrance and went for a walk — catching up, practising their English, talking about everything at once.

Both of their English had improved — you could tell by how the conversation ran without stopping, how each picked up the thread without losing it. Andy noticed mistakes gently, almost invisibly — just repeated the phrase correctly in response, without calling it out. Good method.

Time with him flew just like the first time — quickly and without effort. Until it really was time to part ways.

· · ·
II
Andy and Soho Square

Andy decided to show them what ordinary tours don't show — Soho Square. An entertainment complex at the edge of the resort, alive and noisy even late in the evening. Not a beach, not an excursion — just a place where you could walk around and look, and that was enough.

The Ice Bar was there too — a bar made entirely of ice. Ice walls, ice counter, ice glasses. At the entrance they handed out warm robes — over your clothes, no option, otherwise you wouldn't last long inside. The temperature was low enough that your breath became visible.

They went in not for the drinks — for the place itself. Stood there. Looked around. Touched the walls — cold, frosted, real. Outside was Egyptian night, warm and dark, and inside was a small piece of a completely different climate.

Roy got a photo at the counter with the blue lighting — just to have one.

· · ·

The rest of the evening they spent in Soho — wandering, watching people, listening to music that came from everywhere at once. Andy knew every corner of the place and showed it without hurrying, the way you show something that's yours.

They got back late. It was one of those evenings that don't get planned and for exactly that reason come out better than anything that does.

· · ·
III
Hello Dear Dump

The days passed easily — sea, sun, Andy when you wanted company, honeycomb in the mornings. Roy swam as much as he wanted, dived wherever looked interesting, didn't think about anything serious. Iva was there — calm, content, at home.

The second time turned out to be what it should have been — not a repeat of the first, but its continuation. The first time was a discovery. This one was coming home, except to a home that isn't yours but where you feel good.

· · ·

They packed with that particular feeling that comes on the last day of a good holiday — everything's in order, everything's ready, and at the same time you don't want it to end. Souvenirs for everyone at home took up half the luggage — an unchanged tradition.

The flight back went without surprises. No Budapest, no circling over the airport — just four hours and a landing. Roy even felt a faint nostalgia for that absurd night in a foreign airport. Good stories always carry a little sadness when they end.

After landing he took out his phone and posted one line — short, honest, with the tone that comes when you return from somewhere good to somewhere familiar:

Hello dear dump, I'm back.

Home was good too. Just differently.
