I
The Third Version

The system wasn't built for Klim. It was built for the average — for those who fit within the allotted time, sit quietly, wait for the bell. Klim didn't fit. Not out of spite, just because he couldn't. His head worked faster than the curriculum assumed.

From his first years in school it was always the same. A test — two versions, one per student. Klim finished the first, then the second, then stared at the ceiling and realised there was still half the lesson left with absolutely nothing to do. The solution came naturally: he'd crawl under the desk and start tapping his ruler — quietly, rhythmically, with the satisfaction of someone who had done their part.

The teachers would call Asya. She'd come in, hear the complaints, and shrug.

Then give him a third version. What do you want from me?

They never gave him a third version. The ruler kept tapping.

· · ·

By seventh grade Klim had broken through the system entirely. He had a friend — Tim — who had individual tuition out of necessity. One on one with a teacher, fewer classes, no classroom around him. Klim looked at that arrangement and decided it suited him.

He told Asya simply: either this or nothing.

Asya was a smart woman who had known her son for a long time. She went and made it happen.

From seventh grade Klim came to school one on one with a teacher — perfectly healthy, no grounds for it, just a person who knew what he wanted and knew how to get it. Fewer lessons. More time. The system lost without a word.

II
Go to Hell

Fifth grade. Russian language class. Klim sat there bored — his default state in most lessons. The teacher knew it, Klim knew it, and between them there had long been an unspoken tension of two people who don't particularly like each other.

That day something tipped — maybe homework hadn't been done, maybe moods just aligned. The teacher saw her opportunity. In front of the whole class she started dressing him down, pressing, making an example of him — the kind of boy you shouldn't be. The calculation was clear: public shame, a lowered head, silence.

Klim didn't lower his head.

He looked at her, waited for a pause — and told her exactly where to go. Loudly. In front of everyone. Without hesitation and without regret.

The class went still.

Everything that followed was standard — the principal's office, a talking-to, Asya called in. Klim sat and waited while the adults decided what to do with him. Inside there was no fear, no remorse — just the calm of someone who said what they meant.

· · ·

Strangely, the story had an unexpected ending. That same teacher — the one he'd told off, the one who'd caused a scandal that echoed down the hallway — eventually became one of the few people in the school who genuinely understood him. Something in that directness apparently got through to her. Respect shows up in strange places.

Klim wasn't surprised. He'd noticed long ago: people who try to break you publicly are often the same ones who end up looking at you differently later. The key is not to bend when it matters.

III
Ratatouille and Afghanistan

Ninth grade brought the state exams — taken within the school itself. Biology was on the list. Unpleasant on its own, but not catastrophic. The catastrophe was the teacher.

They didn't get along. Hadn't for a long time, consistently, mutually. The teacher was one of those people whose head is full of their own convictions, and any deviation from them reads as a personal offence. Klim deviated from her on every count.

When the exam papers were handed out it emerged: the whole class got versions one and two. Klim and one other unlucky classmate got a third — assembled by the teacher herself, by hand, on purpose. A Frankenstein stitched together from the most uncomfortable questions, with a single aim: to sink them.

Klim picked up the sheet. Read it. Set it down.

Then picked up his pen and started writing.

· · ·

The final task called for an extended written answer. This was where the teacher expected failure — a blank page, or a jumbled string of words, or a desperate attempt to guess the right answer.

Klim wrote a story. About the war in Afghanistan. About how he personally took part in it — with details, with geography, with a timeline. About the sewers he crawled through under fire. And about his faithful companion — a pet rat named Ratatouille, who had been by his side through every trial and never once let him down.

He wrote from the heart. With detail. With character.

The teacher read it in silence. What was going through her head at that moment is unknown. But by all accounts the Afghanistan story made its way around the staff room fairly quickly.

· · ·

Klim got his grade and went home.

The system wanted to sink him. He wrote about a rat in the sewers and came out dry.

It wasn't rebellion — it was a signature. The kind that doesn't change no matter how many times someone tries to correct it.
