I
The Cross

His father gave him the cross. Not the father who was there — the one who wasn't. Biological, distant, almost abstract. A gift from someone he barely knew — but a real gift, gold, with a weight felt beyond the physical.

Ash wore it as a talisman. Not from religion, not from tradition — it simply became that. The cross was his. Not anyone else's, specifically his — in the way things become part of a person not because they're valuable but because they carry something inside. A story, a person, a moment.

It seemed to Ash that it was needed by everyone. Protection, luck, something that holds. But it belonged only to him. That mattered — having something that was only yours, when so much around was shared or foreign or undefined.

The cross hung on his neck for a long time. Through good and bad, through years that changed, through people who came and went. It stayed — as a reminder that someone, somewhere, once gave something real.

II
Two Thousand Bucks for a Cigarette

There's a song about a dispatcher who gives two thousand dollars for a cigarette. Not because the cigarette is worth that. But because in a specific moment, in a specific state — that's exactly what was needed, and the price stopped mattering.

Ash made it real. Not metaphorically — literally.

The state was what it's like when your head stops working the way it should — without warning, without logic, without the inner dispatcher who usually says stop. Psychosis isn't the drama from films. It's a quiet loss of bearings — when you don't know which way is up, which is down, what matters and what doesn't. Everything merges into one thing and movement starts on its own.

He was alone. Outside. Things were flying — not in a burst of anger, but in that particular state where getting rid of objects feels like getting rid of weight. Phone, documents, money — everything he had on him was going somewhere. Not thrown intentionally — simply released. His hands were doing their thing, his head wasn't stopping them.

· · ·

A person walked by. Random, ordinary — just going about their business. Ash stopped them.

Asked for a cigarette.

They gave one.

III
A Cigarette and a Lighter

The cross came off his neck by itself — or it felt that way. Ash held it out to the stranger. Gold in exchange for a cigarette and a lighter. No negotiation, no explanation, no pause to think it over.

The stranger took it. Handed over the cigarette. Handed over the lighter. Left.

Ash lit up.

· · ·

Then his head started coming back — slowly, the way everything comes back that didn't leave all at once. And with it came the understanding of what had happened. The cross was gone. A gift from a father who hadn't been there. A talisman that had been only his.

And — nothing.

Not emptiness, not pain. Simply nothing — the way it is when you let go of something heavy and realise it was heavier than it seemed. The cross didn't only carry protection — it carried a question without an answer. A person who wasn't there. A relationship that didn't fully exist.

The cigarette burned down. The lighter stayed in his pocket.

· · ·

Everything that holds you back — you can let go. In different ways. Sometimes consciously, with ceremony and the right words. Sometimes like this — alone outside, in a state where the head doesn't filter, and gold goes for a cigarette.

The result is the same. It gets lighter.

Not immediately — but it does.
