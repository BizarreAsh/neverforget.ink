I
The Old Man

They met through a sniper. Not metaphorically — literally. An unknown player in DayZ was shooting at everyone in Zelenogorsk, and that's what brought two groups into the same hangar on the edge of town. Sol's group with Dima — and Berkut's group. They stopped shooting at each other, exchanged a look, swapped contacts.

Then the groups merged. Then became one team. Then Berkut simply became one of theirs — no title, no explanation needed.

The age gap was over ten years. Sol called him the old man — not out of disrespect, but with the particular warmth that only exists when a nickname is exactly right. Berkut was an old man in the best sense: knew a lot, said what mattered, knew how to be there without unnecessary words. By twenty-five he'd already lived enough not to fuss where younger people fussed.

· · ·

They started playing everything together. Not because they were specifically looking for company — it just turned out that playing with Berkut made you want to keep playing. He could hold a conversation, knew how to ease tension, never turned small things into big ones. Sol eventually got him hooked on Minecraft — Berkut resisted for a long time, thought it was childish. Then he got sucked in. The way it always goes with the ones who resist the longest.

Khob appeared naturally — Berkut's friend from offline life, they lived in the same city. A cobbler by trade, a craftsman at his work — whatever that sounds like. The three of them would gather at Berkut's from time to time — to drink, to talk, to spend time. Sol thought of his own people and smiled: everyone has the same thing, just different cities.

II
Falls Asleep Mid-Raid

Berkut had a trait. Not a flaw — a trait, his own, signature. He could fall asleep at any moment — without warning, without announcement, he'd simply go. Mid-raid, at the base, while mining resources. The signal was a gentle snore starting to come through the microphone.

If Berkut stopped moving late in the evening — that was it, the trio risked becoming a duo. The guys knew this and tried to keep him engaged — gave him tasks, asked questions, just kept talking. In non-critical situations they didn't interfere — quietly laughing among themselves every time as if it were the first.

There were cases when he fell asleep at the very start of a raid. Just sat down and went — in the middle of the open world, in a danger zone, with a full inventory. Sol and Nik looked at this not knowing whether to laugh or continue the raid without him. Usually they did both.

· · ·

The rest of the time Berkut was more productive than most. He loved processes that need minimal attention and constant key-pressing — and understood macros well enough that the system pressed the right buttons for him at the right moment. If something needed automating with keyboard or mouse — you went to Berkut.

He worked as a security guard — long shifts, few events. That gave him an extensive film library which he rewatched ten to twenty times each. He knew them by heart — quotes, scenes, details most people miss on a first viewing.

Fishing was his separate world. He'd get in the car and drive — alone, with his own thoughts, with a rod and more patience than most people Sol had ever known. While the car was there — he went often.

· · ·
III
While He's Still in the Game

Berkut at full strength was second to none. Not in games, not in conversation, not in how he showed up when it was needed. He always supported — not with words people say just to say something, but with presence. Simply being there was enough.

Solid through and through. You couldn't find anything bad to say — that's just how it was. Not perfect, just honest. Not loud, just real.

· · ·

There are things said between the lines — not because they're hard to say, but because some words mean more when they aren't said out loud. Berkut knows what this is about. Sol knows. That's enough.

His reserves aren't what they used to be. You notice it — in how he moves, in how quickly he tires, in the fact that there's less fishing now. But he's still here. Still in the game — maybe not at the old speed, but in the game.

People like this don't get forgotten. Not because they did anything special — but because they simply were there. Close, genuinely, without conditions.

Berkut is a legend in his own way.

That's the truth.
