I
Orange and Black

Nora signed Klim up for the team — on the advice of Aunt Tanya, who understood that a boy needs somewhere to put his energy. The logic was simple: if energy doesn't find an outlet in sport it finds one in something else, and that something else isn't always harmless. Klim didn't resist much. Not because he wanted to play football — it just happened that way, and he went.

The kit was orange and black. Vivid, with character, not the grey faceless gear that gets handed out when budgets run short. On the back — a number. Five. Not chosen specifically, it just came to him — but the number stuck. Something in it felt right, recognizable, as if it was always supposed to be his.

Training happened in a hall — small, with a ceiling that pressed down slightly lower than you'd like, smelling of old wood and sweat soaked into the walls over decades. Soviet parquet underfoot — dark, creaking in certain spots, with the streaks of years of polish. That kind of floor remembers a lot of people before you.

· · ·

Klim was a goalkeeper. That's a position for a particular kind of person — not for those who want to run and score, but for those prepared to stand and hold. Psychologically it's different: you're the last line, nothing should get through you, and every conceded goal is yours personally. It's pressure not everyone handles.

Klim handled it — in his own way. Not because he was a natural goalkeeper. Simply because he had a character that didn't know how to give up without a reason. That's not the same as talent — but sometimes it works just as well.

· · ·
II
Soviet Parquet

There was a match. Ordinary — not a final, not a tournament, just a game in the hall against someone or other, it doesn't matter who anymore. What matters is that at some point the ball came toward the goal and Klim had to make a decision.

The decision was: don't fall.

Not out of fear of pain — Soviet parquet is not a soft surface, falling on it means your knees, elbows, palms taking the hit. Something in his head just fired its own way — a principle, stubbornness, or simply the refusal to do what was expected of him in exactly this moment in exactly this way. The ball went in. Goal.

The coach looked at him for a long time. No shouting — just looked. That's worse than shouting.

· · ·

Nora found out about it later — not immediately, gradually, through conversations and hints that built into a picture. Klim didn't make excuses. Explaining what had actually happened in that moment — that he'd decided not to fall not out of cowardice but out of a principle he couldn't yet put into words — was hard. Easier to say nothing.

Silence always came more naturally to him than explanations.

· · ·
III
Experience Gained

Leaving the team was the way most departures are — not one decision but several small ones that at some point added up to a single thing. The coach was pulling him out more and more — not performing well enough, not fitting what a goalkeeper was supposed to be according to this particular coach in this particular hall. Klim saw it and understood.

And at the same time he felt inside that he didn't need it. Not football as such — just this. These training sessions, this hall, this orange-and-black kit that was still a good-looking kit. Something said this wasn't his story, not his road, that the real thing for him was somewhere else.

The computer at home was waiting. There everything was his own, clear, honest about results. You didn't have to fall on Soviet parquet there to prove you were a goalkeeper.

· · ·

Football left something. Not victories and not defeats — a different kind of experience. The ability to hold a position. The understanding that you're the last and that means nothing should get through you. The habit of staying where you stand even when it presses down.

That came in useful later — not on a pitch, in life. There too there are moments when you need to stand in goal and not fall. And the floor there can be harder than Soviet parquet.

Number five stayed somewhere in memory — orange and black, the smell of an old hall, a creaking floor underfoot.

A good start to a story that went in a completely different direction.
