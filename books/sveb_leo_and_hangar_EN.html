I
Sveb

Sveb was a few years younger — and it showed in exactly one place: his voice. A distinctively high-pitched tone that was impossible to mix up with anyone else on a call. He opened his mouth and you knew immediately who it was, no questions needed. Bis knew this and occasionally used it to his advantage — get Sveb into the chat, and the atmosphere shifted.

No bad habits — which partly explained the voice. Smart, could hold a conversation, found the good in situations where others only saw the bad. They'd fail a mission — "whoa, let's go again" — zero negativity. That was his mode of operation, not performed optimism but just his character.

Sveb was the connective tissue. Not in the sense of deliberately linking everyone — it just worked out that way. He knew a lot of people, crossed paths with many, knew how to be where he was needed. Through him Bis met people who stayed for a long time — not all at once, gradually, one by one.

· · ·

Hundreds of hours together — in MTA, in other games, in calls where they talked about everything and nothing at the same time. Sveb knew how to be there without making noise — he simply showed up, and that was enough.

Then his interests started shifting — DayZ, Arma 2, other directions. Bis watched this movement from the side, until one day he followed. The way it usually goes with those who go first — they don't always know they're clearing the path.

· · ·
II
Leo

Sveb brought Leo. Complete opposite — calm, measured, spoke more slowly and deliberately. Where Sveb was the emotional centre, Leo was the stabilising force. They complemented each other so precisely it seemed deliberate — though nobody had deliberately arranged anything.

Leo's home situation was complicated — sometimes with his mum, sometimes his dad. Didn't drink, didn't smoke. A fairly ordinary guy with qualities that didn't show themselves right away.

Leo's main quality was one — he was the first in the group to understand that money is worth spending on experiences. Not on things, not on status — on shared memories, on what you'll still remember. In MTA they always had the best bases and the coolest cars specifically because Leo didn't hold back on that. His standard line when an expensive purchase came up was short:

Don't stress about it, I'll cover it.

And he would. No conditions, no keeping score, simply because he could and wanted everyone to have a good time. Sometimes he missed out on the very thing he'd paid for — but that never stopped him.

· · ·

Bis watched this and absorbed it. Not deliberately — it's just how observation works when someone is doing something right. Leo planted a pattern of behaviour in him that stayed for a long time: don't hold back for your own people, even if you end up missing out on the shared joy yourself.

Their connection was particular — closer than with the others. Dima brought energy, Leo brought practicality and resources. The duo worked. Hundreds of hours together, decisions made jointly, and even now there's occasional contact — in the category of "we're in touch but everyone has their own life now." Back then it was different.

· · ·
III
The Hangar

Sveb changed direction — DayZ, Arma 2, servers where everything was more serious and rougher than MTA. Bis followed. Not immediately — first he watched, then tried it himself, then couldn't stop playing.

DayZ back then was raw — interesting as a concept, full of holes everywhere else. Cheaters moved freely, anticheat barely existed. The more enterprising ones conjured the best gear out of thin air and sold it for a few dollars. Bis used this when he couldn't be bothered — equipped himself and Sveb, and they went looking for a fight with whoever was around.

On one such outing they landed in the middle of a large firefight in town. A sniper was working through everyone indiscriminately — no known affiliation, no known position. Rounds were coming in chaotic but accurate. Bis and Sveb kept to the walls, moved in short sprints — and at some point realised they were shooting at the same people from opposite sides.

· · ·

They stopped firing at the same moment. It was someone else's group. They gathered in a hangar, swapped contacts, went their separate ways.

Sveb brought Leo. Leo taught that money is for experiences. A sniper in DayZ led to the people who stayed.

That's how it works — some people lead to others, and nobody knows in advance which ones those will be.
