I
Didn't Like Him From the Start

Rus appeared in class the way people appear when nobody was waiting for them — simply transferred in, and that was that. New, blank slate, no history that everyone already knew. In those situations a class either accepts someone or it doesn't. The class accepted Rus with open arms. Ash watched it from the side and found it amusing — he understood why it was happening, but he didn't join the chorus.

Rus didn't sit right with him from the start. Not because of any specific act — just didn't sit right, instinct. It rarely got things wrong.

Then Rus started mocking Vyach. Vyach was the kind of person Ash didn't let get hurt — without announcements and without reasons that needed to be spelled out. That's just how it was, full stop. A couple of thrown lines — and a proper fight broke out. Serious, real, where each held their own where they were stronger or quicker. The only thing that stopped it was the administration stepping in.

· · ·

Ash had never trained in martial arts. Never learned to fight properly — he just knew how to stand his ground as well as those who had. Opponents didn't scare him, however big or grown they were. One thought always sat in his head: I'll manage. Not every fight ended in a win — but they stayed in the memory of everyone who'd been there.

Rus remembered too. After the fight they somehow ended up side by side. That's how it sometimes works — through conflict, through a test, through two people standing across from each other and neither backing down. There's something in that.

· · ·
II
Always the Centre

Rus was sharp — not smart, specifically sharp. The difference matters. Smart people build, sharp people use. Good-looking, knew how to spin a story, knew how to be liked — by those who bought it. Raised on streets, in company that doesn't ask too many questions and doesn't wait for explanations. A slacker at maximum capacity — but with the charm that hid it long enough.

And he knew how to pull the blanket toward himself — this was a skill worth studying. Any situation, any conversation, any group — somehow everything ended up orbiting around him. Not because he was the centre — he just always took the centre position before anyone had thought to claim it.

· · ·

The group turned bad gradually — not in one day and not through one decision. Ash just found at some point that walks were ending differently than they'd started. That he was coming home in a state that needed hiding. That drugs, alcohol and nights out had become part of what time looked like when you were around Rus.

He stopped sitting home for days on end — that was true. But where he was going instead was worth thinking about. Ash was good at adapting to situations — that was his strength and his vulnerability at the same time. When someone nearby is pulling in a certain direction persistently enough — you can miss how far you've gone.

Rus wasn't evil. That matters — he wasn't running a scheme, didn't want to break anyone. He just lived the way he lived, and people around him lived the same way. It wasn't bad intent — it was simply an orbit. Get into it and you start moving in the same direction.

· · ·
III
Stop

The moment Ash stopped wasn't loud. There was no rupture, no confrontation, no final conversation that settled everything. At some point he looked at where he was heading and understood — no, stop, I'm not going there. No explanation for Rus, no ceremony. Just stop.

That doesn't mean everything was terrible the whole time. There were moments when Rus was genuine — rare, but they existed. Sometimes he was real. More often — not. More often it was something that could be summed up simply: one thing in words, something else entirely in practice. And always first, always in the centre, always with a new idea that pulled a little further than was worth going.

· · ·

Rus didn't stop where Ash stopped. There was no such point for him — or there was, and he walked past it without noticing. Where Ash said stop — for Rus a road opened further. And he took it.

Where it led — Ash found out gradually and not in full. The last that reached him — Rus was on the edge. For a long time, steadily, without any sign of it changing.

Most likely he's no longer alive.

· · ·

This isn't a moral and it isn't a lesson — it simply happened that way. Two people walked alongside each other for a while. At some point the roads split. One stopped, the other didn't.

Rus taught one thing — important and specific. A rule that was kept strictly after that: know where your stop is. Not everyone has one. Not everyone hears it. But if you hear it — listen.

Rest in metal.
